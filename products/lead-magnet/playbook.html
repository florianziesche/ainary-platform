The AI Agent Playbook 01

The Stack

What I actually use. No affiliate links. Just what works.

The Orchestration Layer

At the center sits OpenClaw — an open-source AI agent framework that connects to messaging (Telegram, WhatsApp, Discord), runs background tasks, manages memory, and orchestrates multiple AI models. Think of it as the operating system for your AI team.

The Brain

Claude (Anthropic) handles most reasoning, writing, and code generation. For specific tasks, I also use GPT-4 and Gemini. The model matters less than the system around it.

The Memory

Obsidian serves as the knowledge base — notes, research, project files, all in plain Markdown. The agents can read and write to it. This is how they "remember" context across sessions.

The Automation

n8n handles workflows that connect external services — CRM updates, email triggers, data syncs. The AI agents design the workflows. I deploy them.

The Channels

I talk to my AI team via Telegram. It feels like messaging a colleague. I send a task, get updates, review output — all from my phone or desktop. No special interface needed.

Total cost: ~$50-100/month for API calls + $0 for open-source tools. That's less than one hour of freelancer time for a team that works 24/7.

The AI Agent Playbook 03

5 Rules of Agent Management

What I learned from running AI agents every day.

01

Define "done" before they start

"Write me an email" produces garbage. "Write a 5-sentence email to a CNC manufacturer in Saxony, referencing their Heidenhain machines, offering a 15-minute demo" produces something I can send.

02

Give context, not just instructions

My agents know my business goals, target audience, writing style, and current priorities. Without context, they're just a fancy autocomplete.

03

Review like a manager, not a user

Don't accept the first output. Check for accuracy, tone, and completeness. AI writes fast but doesn't know when it's wrong. That's your job.

04

Build systems, not prompts

A prompt is a one-time instruction. A system is: role + context + quality gates + feedback loops. Systems improve. Prompts don't.

05

Measure outcomes, not output

I tracked "emails written" and felt productive. Then I tracked "emails sent" and felt sick. The metric that matters is always the one that reaches another human.

The biggest mistake: thinking more agents = more results. It doesn't. One well-configured agent beats ten poorly managed ones.

The AI Agent Playbook 04

The Journey

From zero to your own AI Chief of Staff.

Most people ask three questions:

"How do I get an AI Chief of Staff?"

"How do I build a team of agents?"

"Where do I even start?"

Here's the honest path. It's not instant. But it's faster than you think.

W1

Week 1: One Agent, One Task

Pick your most repetitive knowledge task. Prospect research. Email drafting. Meeting prep. Set up OpenClaw with one AI model. Give it a clear role and test it for 5 days. Expect mediocre results. That's normal.

W2

Week 2: Add Memory

Connect your knowledge base (Obsidian, Notion, or plain files). Now your agent has context — who you are, what you're working on, your preferences. Output quality jumps immediately.

W3

Week 3: Build Quality Gates

Define what "good output" looks like. Create checklists. Teach the agent your voice and standards. This is where most people skip — and where the real leverage lives.

W4

Week 4: Your Chief of Staff

Your first agent is now reliable. Promote it. Give it a name, broader context, and the ability to delegate to specialized sub-agents. Congratulations — you have your AI COO.

M2

Month 2+: Scale the Team

Add agents one by one. Researcher. Writer. Outreach. Each with clear roles and quality expectations. Your COO manages them. You manage the COO. The system compounds.

The key insight: you don't build 10 agents. You build 1, make it excellent, then clone the pattern. The management skill transfers perfectly.

What Works. What Doesn't.

The AI Agent Playbook 06

Your Questions, Answered

What people ask most when they see my setup.

Do I need to code?

No. OpenClaw installs with one command. Configuration is guided. If you can use Telegram, you can run an AI agent. Technical skills help with customization, but they're not required to start.

How much does it cost to run?

The tools are free (OpenClaw, Obsidian). You pay for AI API calls — typically $30-100/month depending on usage. That's the cost of one business lunch for a team that works 24/7.

Is my data safe?

OpenClaw runs locally on your machine. Your data doesn't go to a third party. API calls go to the model provider (Anthropic, OpenAI), which have enterprise-grade data policies. You control what the agents can access.

Can AI agents really replace employees?

No. And that's not the point. Agents handle the repetitive, scalable parts of knowledge work — research, drafting, analysis, formatting. The human handles judgment, relationships, strategy, and the "send" button. Together, one person does the work of ten.

What if the output is bad?

It will be, at first. That's expected. The quality of AI output is directly proportional to the clarity of your instructions. Vague input → vague output. Every time. The playbook's quality gates section exists for exactly this reason.

How long until I see results?

Most people save 1-2 hours in the first week on research and drafting alone. Real leverage — the compounding kind — kicks in around week 3-4 when your agent has enough context to produce work you'd actually use without heavy editing.

What's the biggest mistake people make?

Building too many agents too fast. Start with one. Make it excellent. The pattern replicates. The management skill is the bottleneck, not the technology.

The AI Agent Playbook 07

Start Here

Your first AI agent in under an hour.

01

Pick ONE recurring task

Not "run my business." Something specific: research prospects before meetings, draft follow-up emails, summarize industry news. Start small.

02

Write a clear brief

Who is this agent? What do they do? What context do they need? What does "done" look like? Be specific. The more precise your brief, the better the output.

03

Run it 5 times. Review every output.

The first output will be mediocre. That's normal. Review it like you'd review a junior employee's work. Note what's wrong. Adjust the brief.

04

Refine the brief. Repeat.

After 5 iterations, your brief should be tight enough that the agent produces usable output 80% of the time. That's your baseline.

05

Add a second agent only when the first delivers consistently.

Don't build 10 agents on day one. Nail one first. The skills transfer.
