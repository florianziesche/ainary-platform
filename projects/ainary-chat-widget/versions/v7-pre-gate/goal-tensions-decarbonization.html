01 ‚Äî Executive Summary

Key Insight

Family firms don't resolve goal tensions between economic performance and decarbonization ‚Äî they manage them through attention allocation. The firm's motivation (intrinsic vs. extrinsic) determines which strategy it selects, and these strategies are more heterogeneous than existing theory predicts.

Prof. Gunther Friedl's recent research at TUM School of Management investigates one of the most pressing strategic challenges facing firms today: how to balance the tension between economic goals and environmental imperatives ‚Äî specifically decarbonization. E

Using a multiple case study of eleven German family firms and employing the attention-based view (ABV) of the firm, Friedl et al. derive a model connecting motivation ‚Üí attitude ‚Üí strategy for managing goal tensions. 03 ‚Äî Key Findings

F-01

Goal tensions are managed, not resolved

Firms don't eliminate the tension between economic performance and decarbonization. Instead, they develop ongoing management strategies ‚Äî prioritizing, balancing, or integrating the competing goals based on their organizational attention structure.

E 11 case studies
F-02

Motivation is both intrinsic and extrinsic

Traditional theory emphasizes intrinsic motivation in family firms ("it's in the family's genes"). Friedl et al. show that extrinsic factors ‚Äî regulation (EU Green Deal), customer pressure, and competitive dynamics ‚Äî are equally or more important as drivers of environmental commitment. This contradicts the dominant narrative in family business research.

F-03

Attention-based view explains strategy heterogeneity

Why do similar firms choose different decarbonization strategies? The attention-based view (ABV) provides the answer: firms' organizational attention ‚Äî what decision-makers focus on ‚Äî determines which goal tension strategy they adopt. This is shaped by structural factors (board composition, reporting lines) and procedural factors (how information flows to decision-makers).

04 ‚Äî The Goal Tension Framework

Friedl et al.'s model follows a three-stage logic: E

Stage 1: Motivation ‚Äî What drives the firm toward decarbonization?

  • Intrinsic: Family values, legacy orientation, identity ("because it's in our genes")
  • Extrinsic: Regulation, customer demands, competitive pressure, ESG investor requirements
  • Mixed: Most firms exhibit both ‚Äî the ratio shapes the next stage

Stage 2: Attitude toward goal tension ‚Äî How does the firm frame the conflict?

  • Complementary: "Green investment drives long-term profit" ‚Äî sees no fundamental conflict
  • Competing: "Every euro for decarbonization is a euro less profit" ‚Äî zero-sum framing
  • Paradoxical: "Both are true simultaneously" ‚Äî holds the tension

Stage 3: Strategy for balancing ‚Äî What does the firm actually do?

  • Prioritizing: Sequencing goals ‚Äî "profit first, then invest in green"
  • Balancing: Allocating resources to both simultaneously
  • Integrating: Redesigning processes so both goals reinforce each other

Implication for Controlling

This framework directly challenges traditional management accounting: if goal tensions are ongoing (not solvable), then performance measurement systems must accommodate dual objectives permanently ‚Äî not as a temporary project. KPI systems, incentive structures, and reporting need redesign.

The goal tensions framework is studied in family firms. Does it apply to publicly listed companies, state-owned enterprises, or startups? The attention-based view should generalize, but empirical validation is missing.
