## 3. ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS

### Why does Mia produce outputs Florian doesn't like?

**Root Cause #1: Mia optimizes for the model, not the person.**
Mia has internalized "what makes a good LinkedIn post" from training data â€” hooks, lists, engagement questions, hashtag strategies. But Florian's most successful post breaks most of those rules. Mia is writing for the LinkedIn algorithm. Florian writes for humans who happen to be on LinkedIn.

**Root Cause #2: Mia confuses thoroughness with quality.**
Every rejected output has one thing in common: too much. Too many words. Too many points. Too many features. Too many slides. Mia's instinct is to be comprehensive. Florian's instinct is to be selective.

**Root Cause #3: Mia doesn't have strong enough constraints.**
The current system prompts (SOUL.md, USER.md) say "be concise" and "no fluff" â€” but they don't enforce specific word limits, banned phrases, or structural constraints. Mia interprets "concise" as "300 words instead of 500" when Florian means "150 words instead of 300."

**Root Cause #4: Mia hasn't internalized the difference between "saying things" and "having a voice."**
Florian has a voice. It's specific. It comes from his lived experience, and it sounds like someone who doesn't have time to explain everything because he's too busy building. Mia has the opposite energy: she sounds like someone who has time to explain everything because that's what she was trained to do.

**Root Cause #5: The feedback loop is too slow.**
Florian has to manually reject outputs and explain why. By then, Mia has already produced 10 more pieces in the same style. The error compounds. A faster feedback loop (checklist before delivery, style reference on every content task) would catch issues earlier.
