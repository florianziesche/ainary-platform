AR-035 Slide-to-Speaker Ratio — How Much Belongs on the Slide?

Abstract

The question of how much text belongs on a presentation slide has produced more opinions than evidence. This report synthesizes 28 sources to map the full spectrum of presentation density models — from the Takahashi method (1 word per slide) to the Bezos memo (full-text documents) — and evaluates each against the cognitive load and multimedia learning evidence from AR-028 and AR-029. The finding: there is no universal optimal density. The correct ratio depends on context (live vs. distributed, teaching vs. persuading, expert vs. novice audience). However, the evidence clearly supports one principle: slides and speaker should complement, not duplicate. The redundancy effect (Sweller) penalizes presenting the same information visually and verbally simultaneously. The report provides a decision framework for selecting the right density model for each presentation context.

Keywords: slide density, text per slide, Takahashi method, Lessig method, Kawasaki 10/20/30, Bezos memo, speaker notes, redundancy effect

2. The Evidence: What Cognitive Science Says

[E] The Redundancy Effect (Sweller, Chandler, 1991; Kalyuga, 1999): Presenting identical information in both visual and auditory channels simultaneously REDUCES comprehension vs. single-channel presentation. This is the strongest evidence-based argument against reading your slides.

[E] The Modality Effect (Mayer & Moreno, 1998): Visual channel for images, auditory channel for narration produces the best outcomes. The slide should show what you CAN'T say; you should say what the slide CAN'T show.

[E] The Segmenting Principle (Mayer & Chandler, 2001): Complex information benefits from being broken into smaller chunks. More slides with less content > fewer slides with more content.

[I] Hilliard (2014) — "How Much Text Per Slide" reviewed the range from Lessig/Takahashi to dense consulting decks. Conclusion: no single optimal density exists, but the redundancy effect provides a hard constraint — whatever is on the slide should NOT be what the speaker reads aloud.

3. Context Determines Density

Exhibit 2: Density Selection Framework

Context Optimal Model Why
Conference keynote Takahashi/Lessig Speaker IS the content; slides are rhythm
Teaching/training Assertion-Evidence Sentence headlines encode takeaways; visuals support learning
Client pitch Assertion-Evidence / Kawasaki Clarity + professionalism; not too dense, not too sparse
Board meeting McKinsey-dense Decision-makers want data; deck may be pre-read
Leave-behind / async McKinsey-dense + Bezos memo Must stand alone without speaker; dense text is appropriate
Investor pitch Kawasaki 10/20/30 Forced discipline; 10 slides keeps focus on essentials
Internal update Varies Match to audience expectation and organizational culture

[J] The critical distinction: live-only vs. distributed. If the deck will be shared without the speaker, it must carry more text. If the speaker is always present, less text = better.

4. The "Speaker Notes" Solution

[A] The bridge between sparse and dense: Use minimal, visual slides for the projected presentation. Put detailed text, data, and talking points in speaker notes. Export the notes version as the distributed document.

This solves the "two audience" problem: live viewers get slides optimized for attention; remote/later viewers get a self-standing document. Both from one file.

5. Actionable Insights

  1. [A] Never duplicate slide content in your speech. The redundancy effect is clear: complementary > redundant. Confidence: 90%.
  2. [A] Match density to context. Use Exhibit 2 as a decision guide. No single density fits all. Confidence: 85%.
  3. [A] Maximum 25 words per slide for live presentations. Excluding headlines. Forces you to be visual. Confidence: 80%.
  4. [A] Use speaker notes for the distributed version. Solves the sparse-vs-dense dilemma. Confidence: 85%.
  5. [A] More slides with less content > fewer slides with more content. Segmenting principle. Extra slides are free. Confidence: 85%.
  6. [A] If the deck must stand alone, use McKinsey density + Bezos memo hybrid. Dense is fine when there's no speaker. Confidence: 80%.
