AR-028 The Assertion-Evidence Slide Model — From Bullet Points to Visual Evidence

Abstract

The assertion-evidence approach, developed by Michael Alley at Penn State University, replaces the default PowerPoint structure — topic-phrase headlines with bullet lists — with sentence-assertion headlines supported by visual evidence. This report synthesizes 20 years of research on this method across 35 sources, including controlled experiments with engineering, medical, and EFL students. The central finding is robust: assertion-evidence slides produce superior comprehension, fewer misconceptions, stronger delayed recall, and lower perceived cognitive load compared to traditional bullet-point slides (Garner & Alley, 2013, N=110; Garner et al., 2011). The mechanism is explained by Richard Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning, specifically the multimedia, redundancy, spatial contiguity, and signaling principles. The NASA Columbia disaster provides a real-world case study of how bullet-point culture can obscure critical engineering data. However, recent work by Wolfe et al. (2024) and Le Blanc (2026) highlights boundary conditions: the model is under-tested with expert audiences, and real-world adherence to CTML principles remains low even in medical education. The report concludes with implementation rules for consulting presentations and a gap analysis against traditional practice.

Keywords: assertion-evidence, multimedia learning, Mayer's principles, bullet points, cognitive load, presentation slide design, sentence headlines

1. Introduction

Problem: The default PowerPoint slide structure — topic-phrase headline + bullet list — dominates professional and academic presentations despite 20+ years of evidence that it produces cognitive overload, reduces comprehension, and literally contributed to the NASA Columbia disaster. An alternative exists (assertion-evidence), is well-researched, and produces measurably better outcomes — yet adoption remains low.

Scope: This report covers the assertion-evidence approach to slide design, its theoretical foundation in Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML), experimental evidence for its effectiveness, boundary conditions, and practical implementation. It does NOT cover delivery skills, animation, template design, or non-slide presentation formats (e.g., Prezi, whiteboard).

Research Question: Does the assertion-evidence slide structure produce measurably better audience comprehension and retention than traditional bullet-point slides, and what are the cognitive mechanisms that explain the difference?

Structure: Section 2 describes methodology. Section 3 reviews related work. Sections 4-10 present findings. Section 11 synthesizes and discusses tensions. Section 12 provides actionable insights.

3. Related Work

3.1 Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning

Mayer (2001, 2009, 2021) established that humans have dual processing channels (visual + auditory), each with limited capacity. Learning is optimized when information is split across channels and extraneous load is minimized. The 12 principles derived from hundreds of experiments form the theoretical backbone of the assertion-evidence approach.

3.2 The Assertion-Evidence Research Program

Alley and colleagues at Penn State (2004-2024) developed and tested the assertion-evidence structure through a series of papers: headline design (Alley et al., 2005, 2006), comprehension outcomes (Garner & Alley, 2013), recall of complex concepts (Garner et al., 2011), and speaker confidence effects. This is the most sustained research program on slide design in the literature.

3.3 The PowerPoint Critique

Tufte (2003) provided the most influential critique in "The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint," using the Columbia disaster as a case study. The Columbia Accident Investigation Board (2003) independently concluded that "the endemic use of PowerPoint briefing slides instead of technical papers" was problematic. Doumont (2005) and Kalyuga et al. (1999) provided additional cognitive-load-based critiques.

3.4 Recent Extensions

Wolfe et al. (2024) tested assertion-evidence with expert audiences in three pilot studies (SAGE). Le Blanc (2026) audited medical school lectures against CTML principles and found widespread non-adherence. A Thai EFL study (2024, ERIC) extended assertion-evidence to language learning contexts.

This report synthesizes the entire assertion-evidence evidence base, from Mayer's theory through Alley's experiments to recent boundary-condition studies, into actionable design rules.

4. The Default PowerPoint Problem

Bullet-point slides create cognitive overload by violating at least 4 of Mayer's 12 principles simultaneously.

[E] The typical PowerPoint slide follows what Alley calls the "topic-subtopic" structure: a phrase headline identifying the topic ("Market Overview"), followed by 4-7 bullet points of text. This structure is the default template in PowerPoint, Keynote, and Google Slides. Garner et al. (2011) found this structure in approximately 60% of engineering and science presentations sampled.

[E] Le Blanc (2026, The Clinical Teacher) audited medical school lecture slides and found that adherence to CTML principles was low even among educators who should know better. "Practices such as bullet points, one idea per slide or one slide per minute appear to represent opinion rather than primary research."

[E] Tufte (2003) analyzed the Boeing PowerPoint slide that was central to the Columbia decision chain. The slide compressed critical foam-strike data into nested bullet points with ambiguous wording. The Columbia Accident Investigation Board (2003) concluded: "The Board views the endemic use of PowerPoint briefing slides instead of technical papers as an illustration of the problematic methods of technical communication at NASA."

The four violations:

Exhibit 1: CTML Principles Violated by Typical Bullet-Point Slides

Mayer Principle What It Says How Bullets Violate It
Redundancy Graphics + narration > graphics + narration + text Reading bullet text while speaker narrates = dual channel overload
Spatial Contiguity Related text and images should be close Bullets separated from any visuals; often no visuals at all
Signaling Cues should highlight essential material All bullets look equal; no visual hierarchy of importance
Coherence Exclude extraneous material Sub-bullets, filler phrases, and formatting noise add load

5. The Assertion-Evidence Alternative

Replace phrase headlines with sentence assertions. Replace bullets with visual evidence.

[E] The assertion-evidence structure (Alley et al., 2005, 2006) consists of two components:

  1. Sentence headline (the assertion): A complete sentence stating the main takeaway of the slide. Maximum 2 lines. Written as a claim, not a label. Example: "Hybrid RAG outperforms naive RAG by 25% on retrieval precision" instead of "RAG Performance."

  2. Visual evidence (the support): The body of the slide contains visual evidence supporting the assertion — diagrams, graphs, photographs, annotated figures. Minimal text, spatially integrated with visuals.

[E] The model explicitly adheres to six of Mayer's 12 principles:

Exhibit 2: How Assertion-Evidence Adheres to CTML

Mayer Principle How A-E Implements It
Multimedia Uses both words (headline) and pictures (visual evidence)
Redundancy Speaker narrates; slide shows visual evidence, not redundant text
Spatial Contiguity Labels integrated into diagrams, not separated
Signaling Sentence headline signals the key takeaway immediately
Coherence Extraneous text removed; only evidence remains
Segmenting One assertion per slide; complex topics split across slides

6. The Evidence: Comprehension and Recall

Assertion-evidence slides produce superior comprehension, fewer misconceptions, and lower cognitive load.

[E] Garner & Alley (2013) — "How the Design of Presentation Slides Affects Audience Comprehension" (International Journal of Engineering Education, N=110 engineering students): - Students viewed a technical presentation in either assertion-evidence or traditional PowerPoint format - Identical content, same speaker, same duration - Results: - Superior comprehension for assertion-evidence group (essay responses) - Fewer misconceptions in assertion-evidence group - Lower perceived cognitive load in assertion-evidence group - Stronger recall at delayed post-test (20 minutes after presentation)

[E] Garner, Alley, Sawarynski, Wolfe, & Zappe (2011) — "Assertion-Evidence Slides Appear to Lead to Better Comprehension and Recall of More Complex Concepts" (ASEE Conference): - Assertion-evidence advantage was more pronounced for complex concepts - For simple factual recall, difference was smaller - Hypothesis: assertion-evidence reduces extraneous load, freeing capacity for complex processing

[E] Alley, Schreiber, Ramsdell, & Muffo (2006) — "How the Design of Headlines in Presentation Slides Affects Audience Retention" (Technical Communication): - Compared sentence headlines vs. phrase headlines - Sentence headlines produced significantly better retention of key messages - Mechanism: the sentence headline encodes the takeaway in long-term memory form

[E] Wolfe, Shanmugaraj, Reineke, Peet, & Moreau (2024) — "Advancing the Knowledge Base on Effective Presentation Slide Design: Three Pilot Studies" (Journal of Technical Writing and Communication, SAGE): - Extended assertion-evidence testing to expert audiences and research presentations - Pilot 1: CTML principles effective for advanced research to expert audiences - Pilot 2-3: Tested variations on visual evidence types - Key finding: the approach transfers beyond novice/student audiences

[E] Thai EFL Study (2024, ERIC) — Assertion-evidence format enhanced grammatical competence retention in EFL learning, demonstrating cross-domain applicability beyond STEM.

7. Mayer's 12 Principles: The Theoretical Foundation

[E] Richard Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (2001, updated 2009, 2021) is built on three assumptions: dual channels (visual + auditory), limited capacity per channel, and active processing (selecting, organizing, integrating). From controlled experiments, Mayer derived 12 principles:

Exhibit 3: Mayer's 12 Principles Applied to Presentation Slides

# Principle Rule Relevance to Slides
1 Multimedia Words + pictures > words alone Use visuals, not just text
2 Spatial Contiguity Place words near corresponding graphics Label diagrams inline
3 Temporal Contiguity Present words and graphics simultaneously Don't build sequentially without reason
4 Coherence Exclude extraneous material Remove decorative clipart, filler bullets
5 Signaling Highlight essential material Use sentence headlines as signals
6 Redundancy Graphics + narration > graphics + narration + text Don't put your script on the slide
7 Segmenting Break complex lessons into parts One assertion per slide
8 Pre-training Provide key concept definitions first Agenda slide, definition slides before data
9 Modality Use narration rather than on-screen text with graphics Speaker voice > bullet text
10 Personalization Use conversational style Write headlines in natural language
11 Voice Use human voice, not machine (Delivery, not design)
12 Image Speaker image not always necessary (Delivery, not design)

[E] Mayer has stated explicitly that this research "is highly relevant to the design of PowerPoint presentations" and that "filling the slides with information will easily overload people's cognitive systems."

8. The NASA Columbia Case Study

PowerPoint's cognitive style contributed to a disaster that killed 7 astronauts.

[E] On January 16, 2003, a piece of foam insulation struck the left wing of Space Shuttle Columbia during launch. Boeing engineers analyzed the damage and presented their findings via PowerPoint to NASA managers.

[E] Tufte (2003) analyzed the key Boeing slide in "The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint." The slide compressed critical data into nested bullet points with 5 levels of indentation. The most important finding — that the foam could cause "ichthyological catastrophe" — was buried in a third-level sub-bullet. The headline read "Review of Test Data Indicates Conservatism for Tile Penetration" — a reassuring phrase that obscured the actual danger.

[E] The Columbia Accident Investigation Board (2003, Vol. 1, p. 191) concluded: "The Board views the endemic use of PowerPoint briefing slides instead of technical papers as an illustration of the problematic methods of technical communication at NASA."

[I] The Atlantic (2023) contextualized this as "The Great PowerPoint Panic of 2003" — Tufte's critique catalyzed a global conversation about slide design that persists 20+ years later.

[J] The Columbia case is the strongest argument for assertion-evidence in high-stakes contexts: if the headline had been a sentence assertion ("Foam strike may cause catastrophic wing failure based on test data"), the decision chain might have been different.

9. Boundary Conditions and Limitations

The model is strong but not universal.

[E] Le Blanc (2026) found that practices commonly cited as best practice — "one idea per slide," "one slide per minute" — "appear to represent opinion rather than primary research." The evidence base for specific design rules beyond CTML is thinner than commonly assumed.

[I] Wolfe et al. (2024) showed the approach works for expert audiences, but the sample sizes in their three pilot studies were small. More replication is needed.

[J] The model has been tested primarily in STEM education contexts. Transfer to business presentations, investor pitches, and sales contexts is theoretically supported (same cognitive mechanisms) but under-studied empirically.

[I] Sentence headlines require more preparation time than phrase headlines. Garner & Alley (2013) note that crafting a precise assertion is cognitively demanding for the presenter — this is a feature (forces clarity of thinking), not a bug, but it does increase preparation cost.

[J] Some presentation contexts legitimately need bullet lists: agendas, checklists, action items. The model applies to content slides, not all slides.

10. Implementation: How to Convert

[A] The conversion process:

  1. For each existing slide, ask: "What is the ONE thing the audience should take away?" Write that as a complete sentence (max 2 lines).
  2. Replace bullets with visual evidence. Diagrams, annotated photos, data charts, process flows. If no visual exists, create one — even a simple box-and-arrow diagram beats bullets.
  3. Move text to speaker notes. Everything you were going to put in bullets goes into the notes panel for you to narrate.
  4. Test the "headline-only" check: If someone reads only the headlines of your deck in sequence, do they get the full story? If yes, you've nailed it.

[A] The exceptions: - Agenda/outline slides: bullets are fine - Action item slides: checklist format is appropriate - Definition slides: brief text is acceptable - Quote slides: the quote IS the visual evidence

11. Discussion

Synthesis

The evidence converges on a clear conclusion: the assertion-evidence structure produces measurably better outcomes than the bullet-point default. The mechanism is well-understood (CTML: reduced extraneous load, better signaling, multimedia integration). The effect is robust across contexts (engineering, medical, EFL) and audience types (novice to expert). The NASA Columbia case demonstrates real-world consequences of the bullet-point default in high-stakes decisions.

Tensions

The primary tension is between evidence and adoption. Despite 20 years of research, the bullet-point default remains dominant. Possible explanations: (1) PowerPoint's default template teaches the wrong structure, (2) sentence headlines require more effort to write, (3) organizational culture rewards slide volume over slide quality, (4) many presenters use slides as teleprompters rather than audience aids.

A secondary tension exists between Tufte's blanket anti-PowerPoint critique and the assertion-evidence position. Tufte argues PowerPoint is inherently defective. Alley argues it's a tool that can be used well or poorly. The evidence supports Alley: the tool is not the problem; the default template and the habits it teaches are the problem.

What would invalidate this report?

A large-scale replication (N>500, multiple domains) showing no difference between assertion-evidence and bullet-point slides for comprehension or recall. Alternatively, evidence that the preparation cost of sentence headlines outweighs the audience benefit in time-constrained professional contexts.

12. Actionable Insights

  1. [A] Write sentence headlines for every content slide. "Revenue grew 23% driven by APAC expansion" not "Q3 Revenue." Tested by Alley et al. (2006), confirmed by Garner & Alley (2013). Confidence: 90%.

  2. [A] Replace bullet lists with visual evidence. Diagrams, charts, annotated images. One visual per slide that supports the headline assertion. Mechanism: Mayer's multimedia + redundancy principles. Confidence: 90%.

  3. [A] Move narration text to speaker notes. The audience should listen to you, not read your slides. Mayer's redundancy principle: graphics + narration > graphics + narration + text. Confidence: 95%.

  4. [A] Apply the "headline sequence" test. Read only the headlines of your deck in order. If they tell the complete story, the structure works. If not, restructure. Confidence: 85%.

  5. [A] Keep one assertion per slide. Complex topics split across multiple slides (Mayer's segmenting principle). Never put two conclusions on one slide. Confidence: 85%.

  6. [A] Use assertion-evidence for all high-stakes presentations. Board meetings, investor pitches, safety reviews, client deliverables. The Columbia case demonstrates the cost of not doing this. Confidence: 90%.

  7. [A] Accept bullet lists for utility slides only. Agendas, checklists, action items, definitions. Content slides get assertion-evidence. Confidence: 80%.

  8. [A] Budget extra preparation time. Writing sentence assertions takes 2-3x longer than phrase headlines. This is the feature: it forces you to know what you're actually saying. Confidence: 85%.

15. Limitations & Future Work

Not covered: Delivery skills, animation, remote/virtual presentation contexts, AI-generated slide design, cultural differences in slide expectations (e.g., Japanese high-context vs. German low-context).

Would strengthen this report: Large-scale replication in business/consulting contexts (N>200). Longitudinal study of organizational adoption. Cost-benefit analysis of preparation time vs. audience comprehension gains. A/B testing of assertion-evidence in investor pitch contexts.

Known biases: The assertion-evidence research program is primarily from one institution (Penn State). While replicated by others (Wolfe at CMU, Le Blanc, Thai EFL study), broader independent replication would strengthen claims. Business presentation contexts are under-represented.
