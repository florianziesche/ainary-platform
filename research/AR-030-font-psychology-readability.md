# AR-030 Font Psychology & Readability in Presentations — What the Research Actually Shows

## Abstract

Typography in presentations operates at two levels: legibility (can the audience physically read the text?) and rhetoric (what does the typeface communicate beyond the words?). This report synthesizes 36 sources across perceptual psychology, typographic research, and applied design to separate evidence from convention. The central finding on the serif vs. sans-serif debate is definitive: at presentation-scale sizes (24pt+), the difference in legibility is negligible — both are equally readable (Arditi & Cho, 2005; Moret-Tatay & Perea, 2011). What matters more is x-height ratio, stroke contrast, and inter-character spacing. On font personality, Brumberger (2003) and Mackiewicz & Moeller (2004) established that typefaces carry consistent personality impressions (professional, friendly, authoritative) that are perceived independently of content. The disfluency effect — the claim that hard-to-read fonts improve memory (Diemand-Yauman et al., 2011) — has failed to replicate consistently and should not guide presentation design. OpenDyslexic fonts show no measurable benefit for dyslexic readers in controlled studies. The report concludes with evidence-based font selection rules for presentations, mapped to audience context and viewing distance.

**Keywords:** typography, font psychology, serif, sans-serif, x-height, readability, legibility, disfluency effect, typeface personality, presentation design

Meanwhile, typography research has produced actionable findings that most presenters have never encountered — including the fact that the most commonly cited rule (serif vs. sans-serif) barely matters at presentation sizes.

**Scope:** Font properties affecting legibility and readability at presentation scale (24pt+). Typeface personality perception and its effect on credibility. The disfluency effect. ## 3. Related Work

### 3.1 The Legibility Tradition
Tinker (1963) established foundational legibility research for print. Arditi and Cho (2005) modernized this for screen displays. Perea and Moret-Tatay (2011) addressed serif vs. sans-serif for digital reading. Rello and Baeza-Yates (2016) extended to font size and line spacing for screen readability.

### 3.2 Font Personality Research
Poffenberger and Franken (1923) first demonstrated that typefaces carry emotional connotations. Brumberger (2003) established "The Rhetoric of Typography" as a framework. Mackiewicz and Moeller (2004) connected specific typeface features to personality traits. Juni and Gross (2008) tested emotional and persuasive font effects.

### 3.3 The Disfluency Debate
Diemand-Yauman, Oppenheimer, and Vaughan (2011) claimed that hard-to-read fonts improve retention through "desirable difficulty." Multiple replication attempts have produced mixed results. French et al. (2013) and Rummer et al. (2016) failed to replicate. The effect is now considered unreliable.

### 3.4 Accessibility Typography
Rello and Baeza-Yates (2013) tested 12 fonts with dyslexic readers. Wery and Diliberto (2017) tested OpenDyslexic specifically. Kuster et al. (2018) replicated. Consensus: specialized dyslexia fonts show no measurable benefit over well-designed standard fonts.

## 4. The Serif vs. Sans-Serif Non-Debate

**At presentation sizes, it doesn't matter. Both are equally readable.**

[E] **Arditi & Cho (2005)** — "Serifs and Font Legibility" (Vision Research, PMC): Using controlled fonts varying only in serif size (0%, 5%, 10% cap height), they measured legibility via size thresholds and reading speed. Result: 5% serifs were slightly more legible than sans-serif, but the difference was within inter-letter spacing variability. **Serifs per se do not hurt or help legibility in any meaningful way.**

[E] **Moret-Tatay & Perea (2011)** — Found no significant difference in reading times between serif (Times New Roman) and sans-serif (Arial) at standard screen sizes.

[E] **De Lange et al. (2022)** — "The Dispute About Sans Serif vs Serif Fonts" (Acta Psychologica, ScienceDirect): Demonstrated that the serif/sans-serif variable interacts with stroke contrast. The perceived difference is driven more by stroke thickness variation than by serifs themselves.

[E] **Lyngo Lab (N=1,200)** — "Certain serif fonts may be slightly more difficult to read than sans serif fonts." But the effect is small and font-specific — not a category-level finding.

[I] **The resolution:** The serif vs. sans-serif debate has persisted for decades because researchers kept comparing different fonts (Times vs. Arial) instead of controlling for individual variables (x-height, weight, spacing). When variables are properly controlled, serifs contribute almost nothing to legibility at sizes above 14pt. At presentation sizes (24pt+), the difference is effectively zero.

[A] **Presentation rule:** Choose fonts based on x-height, weight, and spacing — not serif presence. Both Inter (sans-serif) and Source Serif Pro work fine at 24pt+. Stop debating serif vs. sans-serif.

## 5. What Actually Affects Legibility

**x-height, stroke contrast, spacing, and size matter. Serifs don't.**

[E] The typographic variables that measurably affect legibility, ranked by impact:

*Exhibit 1: Legibility Factors Ranked by Evidence Strength*

| Factor | Impact on Legibility | Evidence | Presentation Implication |
|--------|---------------------|----------|------------------------|
| **Font size** | ●●●●● | Rello (2016): size is single strongest predictor of reading speed on screen | Minimum 24pt body, 30pt+ headlines |
| **x-height ratio** | ●●●●○ | Fonts with higher x-height (lowercase height relative to cap height) are more legible at smaller sizes | Choose fonts with high x-height: Inter, Verdana, Roboto |
| **Inter-character spacing** | ●●●●○ | Arditi (2005): spacing variability accounts for more legibility variance than serifs | Don't use condensed fonts; slightly increased tracking helps |
| **Stroke contrast** | ●●●○○ | De Lange (2022): high stroke contrast reduces legibility, especially at small sizes | Avoid Didot, Bodoni for slide text; uniform-weight fonts preferred |
| **Line spacing (leading)** | ●●●○○ | Rello (2016): 1.4-1.8× line spacing optimal | 1.5× line-height minimum for slide text |
| **Color contrast** | ●●●●● | WCAG 2.1: 4.5:1 minimum | See AR-027, Section 9 |
| **Serif presence** | ●○○○○ | Arditi (2005), De Lange (2022): negligible at >14pt | Irrelevant for presentation design decisions |

[E] **Rello & Baeza-Yates (2016)** — "Make It Big! The Effect of Font Size and Line Spacing on Online Readability": Font size is the single most impactful variable. Going from 12pt to 18pt improved reading speed by ~14%. Line spacing of 1.4-1.8× was optimal.

[A] **The practical rule:** At presentation sizes (24pt+), nearly any well-designed font is legible. The real failures come from insufficient size (< 24pt), compressed spacing, low contrast against background, and thin/decorative fonts.

## 6. Font Personality: Typefaces Communicate Beyond Words

**Fonts carry personality. The audience perceives it whether you intend it or not.**

[E] **Brumberger (2003)** — "The Rhetoric of Typography: The Persona of Typeface and Text" (Technical Communication): Demonstrated that readers attribute consistent personality traits to typefaces: professional, friendly, authoritative, creative, elegant. These perceptions are stable across readers and independent of content. Participants rated fonts on 15 personality dimensions with high inter-rater reliability.

[E] **Mackiewicz & Moeller (2004)** — "Why People Perceive Typefaces to Have Different Personalities" (IAPR): Identified specific design features that drive personality perception:
- **Weight (bold)** → strength, authority
- **Curved forms** → friendly, approachable
- **Angular forms** → professional, rigid
- **Handwriting style** → warm, personal, less professional

[E] **Juni & Gross (2008)** — "Emotional and Persuasive Perception of Fonts" (SAGE, Perceptual and Motor Skills): Tested satirical text in two fonts. The font influenced perceived seriousness, humor, and persuasiveness of identical content.

*Exhibit 2: Font Category Personality Map*

| Font Category | Example | Perceived Personality | Best For |
|--------------|---------|----------------------|----------|
| Geometric sans-serif | Inter, Helvetica, Futura | Clean, modern, neutral, corporate | Data-heavy presentations, tech |
| Humanist sans-serif | Gill Sans, Frutiger, Calibri | Warm, approachable, readable | Client-facing, workshops |
| Serif (traditional) | Times, Garamond, Baskerville | Authoritative, established, academic | Research reports, legal |
| Slab serif | Rockwell, Clarendon, Roboto Slab | Strong, bold, industrial | Headlines, impact statements |
| Handwriting/Script | Comic Sans, Zapfino, Brush Script | Casual, personal, unprofessional (in business) | Never in business presentations |
| Monospace | JetBrains Mono, Consolas, Courier | Technical, precise, code | Code displays, terminal UI |

[I] **The congruence effect:** Doyle & Bottomley (2006, ScienceDirect) showed that font-product congruence improves brand perception. The same applies to presentations: a font that matches the content's tone amplifies the message; a font that contradicts it creates dissonance.

[A] **Presentation rule:** Choose fonts deliberately for personality, not just legibility. A consulting deck for a bank should use different typography than a startup pitch. Match the font personality to the brand and audience expectations.

## 7. The Disfluency Effect: Debunked for Presentations

**Hard-to-read fonts do NOT improve retention in reliable research.**

[E] **Diemand-Yauman, Oppenheimer, & Vaughan (2011)** — "Fortune Favors the Bold (and the Italicized)" (Cognition): Original claim that material presented in disfluent fonts (Comic Sans Italicized, Haettenschweiler) was better retained than material in fluent fonts (Arial). N=28 (lab) + N=222 (field). The mechanism: disfluency triggers deeper processing.

[E] **French et al. (2013)** — "Disfluency in Flashcard Learning" (multiple experiments): Failed to replicate the disfluency effect. No significant difference in retention between fluent and disfluent fonts.

[E] **Rummer et al. (2016)** — Five experiments, none replicated the disfluency effect. Concluded: "The evidence for disfluency effects on learning is weaker than commonly assumed."

[E] **Taylor & Francis (2020)** — "A Review of the Cognitive Effects of Disfluent Typography on Functional Reading": Meta-review found mixed results across studies. Effect appears in some memorization tasks but not in comprehension tasks. Not reliable enough for design recommendations.

[J] **Verdict for presentations:** Do not use hard-to-read fonts to "improve retention." The disfluency effect is unreliable, has failed multiple replications, and even if real, would be outweighed by the cognitive load cost (see AR-029). Use clear, legible fonts.

## 8. Dyslexia Fonts: Good Intentions, No Evidence

**OpenDyslexic and similar fonts do not help dyslexic readers.**

[E] **Wery & Diliberto (2017)** — "The Effect of a Specialized Dyslexia Font, OpenDyslexic, on Reading Rate and Accuracy" (PMC): Alternating treatment design with students diagnosed with dyslexia. Result: **No improvement in reading rate or accuracy** with OpenDyslexic vs. standard fonts.

[E] **Kuster et al. (2018)** — "Dyslexie Font Does Not Benefit Reading in Children With or Without Dyslexia" (PMC): N=170 children. OpenDyslexic provided no measurable benefit over Arial.

[E] **Rello & Baeza-Yates (2013)** — Tested 12 fonts with dyslexic readers. Found that **standard sans-serif fonts (Arial, Helvetica, Verdana) performed as well or better** than specialized dyslexia fonts. Key finding: font size and inter-word spacing matter more than font design for dyslexic readers.

[E] **Edutopia (2022)** summary of research: OpenDyslexic actually "reduced reading speed and accuracy" compared to Arial and Times New Roman in controlled testing.

[A] **Presentation rule:** Don't use OpenDyslexic. If accessibility for dyslexic readers is a concern, use well-designed sans-serif fonts (Inter, Verdana, Arial) at ≥24pt with generous spacing. Font choice matters less than size and spacing.

## 9. Font Size: The Non-Negotiable Minimum

**24pt body, 30pt+ headlines. No exceptions for projected presentations.**

[E] Viewing distance fundamentally determines minimum font size. Dave Paradi's viewing distance research and practitioner consensus converge on:

*Exhibit 3: Font Size Guidelines by Context*

| Context | Body Text Min | Headlines Min | Annotations Min |
|---------|--------------|---------------|----------------|
| Conference room (3-5m) | 24pt | 30pt | 18pt |
| Auditorium (10-20m) | 28pt | 36pt | 20pt |
| Webinar/screen share | 20pt | 28pt | 16pt |
| Printed handout | 10pt | 14pt | 8pt |

[I] **Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 Rule:** No text smaller than 30pt in any presentation. While overly restrictive, it enforces a useful discipline: if you can't fit it at 30pt, you have too much text.

[A] **The real test:** Stand at the back of the room where you'll present. If you can't read every word on every slide, increase the font size until you can. If you can't fit the content at readable sizes, you have a content problem, not a font problem.

## 10. Font Pairing: Hierarchy Through Typography

[I] **The two-font rule:** Use maximum two fonts in a presentation — one for headlines, one for body text. More than two creates visual noise that adds extraneous cognitive load (see AR-029).

[I] **Effective pairing strategies:**
- **Same family, different weights:** Inter Light (body) + Inter Bold (headlines). Safest option.
- **Complementary contrast:** Serif headline + sans-serif body (or vice versa). Creates visual hierarchy through category contrast.
- **Avoid similarity:** Don't pair two sans-serifs that look almost-but-not-quite the same (Helvetica + Arial). The near-similarity creates perceptual confusion.

*Exhibit 4: Recommended Presentation Font Pairs*

| Pair | Headlines | Body | Personality |
|------|----------|------|-------------|
| Modern corporate | Inter SemiBold | Inter Regular | Clean, neutral, data-friendly |
| Authoritative report | Source Serif Pro | Inter Regular | Established yet readable |
| Tech/startup | JetBrains Mono | Inter Regular | Technical, precise |
| Warm consultancy | Frutiger Bold | Frutiger Light | Approachable, professional |

## 11. Case Sensitivity and Formatting

[E] **ALL CAPS reduces reading speed by 13-20%** compared to mixed case (Tinker, 1963; replicated by Pelli et al., 2006). ALL CAPS eliminates word-shape cues (ascenders/descenders) that aid rapid recognition.

[A] **Presentation rule:** Never set body text in ALL CAPS. Reserve all-caps for short labels only (maximum 3-4 words). Headlines in title case or sentence case.

[E] **Bold text** increases perceived authority (Mackiewicz, 2004) but reduces reading speed when overused. Use bold for emphasis on 1-2 words, not entire sentences.

[I] **Italic text** is harder to read on projectors due to sub-pixel rendering issues. Avoid for projected presentations; acceptable for print/PDF.

## 12. Discussion

### Synthesis
Typography in presentations resolves into two distinct problems: legibility (physical readability) and rhetoric (personality communication). For legibility, the evidence is clear: size > spacing > x-height >> serif presence. For rhetoric, fonts carry measurable personality traits that should match the content and audience. The disfluency effect and dyslexia fonts are both examples of appealing ideas that failed empirical testing.

### Tensions
The primary tension is between typographic minimalism (one neutral font for everything) and typographic expressiveness (using font personality as a communication tool). For most business presentations, minimalism wins — Inter or a similar geometric sans-serif at proper sizes solves 95% of legibility problems. The remaining 5% is for presentations where brand personality matters enough to justify deliberate font choices.

A secondary tension: the evidence base is fragmented across print, web, and presentation contexts. Most legibility studies test at body-text sizes (10-14pt), not presentation sizes (24pt+). At presentation scale, most findings converge to "it doesn't matter much" — which is itself a useful finding.

### What would invalidate this report?
A large-scale study showing that serif vs. sans-serif choice significantly affects comprehension at 24pt+. Alternatively, a successful replication of the disfluency effect in a presentation (not reading) context with N>200.

## 16. Limitations & Future Work

**Not covered:** Variable fonts, responsive typography, web font performance, font rendering differences across operating systems, cultural typography preferences (CJK, Arabic, Devanagari), AI-generated font selection.

**Would strengthen:** Large-scale legibility study at presentation-specific sizes (24pt+) comparing multiple fonts. Cross-cultural font personality study. Interaction between font choice and persuasion (not just comprehension) outcomes.

**Known biases:** English-language fonts only. Most legibility studies use Latin script. Font personality research is Western-centric (Brumberger, Mackiewicz are US-based). Screen-based research may not perfectly transfer to projector-based viewing.
