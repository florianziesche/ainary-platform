## 2. Executive Summary

**90% of AI startups are wrappers. Here's how to find the 10%.**

AI startups captured roughly $89.4 billion [E] in venture capital through 2025, representing approximately one-third of all VC dollars deployed despite being a fraction of funded companies. In H1 2025 alone, AI startups raised $104 billion [E] globally. OpenAI reached a $500 billion valuation [E]; Anthropic hit $183 billion at a $5 billion revenue run-rate [E]. Seventeen US AI startups logged mega-rounds in the first weeks of 2026 alone [E].

The problem: most of this capital is chasing the wrong companies.

The median AI startup pitching today has no proprietary data, no defensible model architecture, and unit economics that collapse the moment their API provider changes pricing. They are, functionally, a prompt template with a landing page. The due diligence frameworks that worked for SaaS in 2018 do not work for AI in 2026. The valuation multiples are different (20x–30x revenue median [E], with outliers exceeding 100x [I]). The risk vectors are different. The technical assessment requires a different vocabulary.

This report provides a 7-layer framework for evaluating AI startups, drawn from hands-on experience at 36ZERO Capital, VC Lab (Decile Group), and building AI ventures from the ground up. It is designed to be used at the deal table—not read and filed.

## 3. The 7-Layer Due Diligence Framework

Most investors evaluate AI companies the same way they evaluate SaaS companies, then wonder why their portfolio is full of commoditized API wrappers. AI requires a different lens. Here are the seven layers, in the order you should assess them.

### Layer 1: Tech Stack Architecture

**What to assess:** Is the company building on top of third-party foundation models (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) or training its own? Where in the stack does their differentiation live?

**Key questions:**
- What percentage of the core product functionality breaks if OpenAI changes its API terms tomorrow?
- Do they own their inference infrastructure, or is it fully cloud-dependent?
- Is there custom model training (fine-tuning, RLHF, distillation), or are they shipping raw API calls with prompt engineering?
- What's the model architecture? Can the CTO whiteboard it without checking notes?

**Benchmark:** Companies with proprietary model layers command 2–3x [I] the revenue multiples of pure API wrappers at comparable revenue stages. EV/EBITDA multiples for robotics and AI companies sat at a median of 16.8x in Q4 2025 [E], but that median masks enormous dispersion based on tech-stack depth.

**Red flag density:** This layer kills 60% [J] of deals in my pipeline before I open a spreadsheet.

### Layer 2: Data Moat

**What to assess:** Data is the only durable competitive advantage in AI that doesn't depreciate with the next model release. But not all data is equal.

**Key questions:**
- Is the data proprietary, licensed, or scraped?
- Does the product generate a data flywheel? (More users → more data → better model → more users)
- Is the data structured, labeled, and ML-ready, or is it a raw dump that requires $2M in cleaning before it's useful?
- What happens to the data if a key partnership ends?

**Framework for data moat scoring:**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Proprietary data generated by product usage, compounding over time |
| 4 | Exclusive licensed data with long-term agreements |
| 3 | Curated public data with significant preprocessing value |
| 2 | Standard datasets available to competitors |
| 1 | No meaningful data asset; model relies entirely on pretrained weights |

Bain's diligence practice now evaluates "defensible data/workflow moats" [E] as a primary deal criterion, noting that companies entrenched in customer workflows with high switching costs and proprietary data assets represent the strongest acquisition targets.

### Layer 3: Team Composition

**What to assess:** AI teams have a specific talent structure that differs from traditional software. A startup with five full-stack developers and zero ML engineers is not an AI company—it's a software company using an API.

**Key signals:**
- Ratio of ML/AI engineers to total engineering headcount. Below 30% [J] in a self-proclaimed "AI company" is a flag.
- Research output: papers, open-source contributions, conference presentations. Not vanity metrics—evidence of depth.
- "Bus factor" analysis: if the lead ML engineer leaves, does the company's technical capability survive? Fast Data Science specifically flags single-point-of-failure team structures [E] as a critical diligence item.
- Founder-market fit: has the founding team worked in the domain they're selling into, or did they pivot into AI because the funding was there?

**Benchmark:** Top-quartile AI startups have a founding team where at least one member has published ML research or shipped production ML systems at scale [I].

### Layer 4: Unit Economics

**What to assess:** AI unit economics are structurally different from SaaS. Inference costs, model training compute, and data acquisition costs create a cost structure that most investors misread.

**Key metrics:**
- **Gross margin:** Traditional SaaS benchmarks (75–85%) don't apply. AI-native companies with heavy inference loads operate at 50–65% [I] gross margins. If someone claims 80%+ gross margins on an AI product, ask where the compute cost is hiding.
- **Inference cost per query/transaction:** Is it declining with scale, or flat? Declining = good architecture. Flat = they're paying per-API-call to a third party.
- **CAC payback period:** AI startups in 2025/2026 showing CAC payback beyond 18 months [I] are burning capital faster than they're building enterprise value.
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR):** Best-in-class AI companies hit 130–150% NRR [I]. Below 110% suggests the product isn't sticky enough to survive competition.

OpenAI reported a $5 billion loss [E] in 2024 on $4 billion revenue [E]. That's the cost structure of a foundation model company. Most AI startups should not have that profile. If an application-layer company is burning at that rate relative to revenue, something is broken.

**The inference cost trap:** A startup showing strong margins today on GPT-4 API calls can see those margins evaporate overnight if OpenAI adjusts pricing. This has happened before. Diligence in 2026 has shifted from "demo sparkle" to verifiable inference cost curves, gross margin uplift from model optimization, and empirical retention among pilot-to-paid conversions [E].

### Layer 5: Market Timing & Size

**What to assess:** Is the market ready for this solution right now—or is the startup three years early with 18 months of runway?

**Key questions:**
- Is the regulatory environment favorable or about to shift? By 2026, responsible AI frameworks covering bias, transparency, and governance are non-negotiable [E] in most PE and strategic transactions.
- Is the buyer persona already spending budget on this problem, or does the startup need to educate the market?
- What's the realistic TAM, not the "if everyone in the world used this" TAM?

**Benchmark:** Global AI spending is projected to exceed $300 billion [I] by end of 2026 across enterprise and consumer segments. But TAM is not a strategy. The question is always: what share of that spending is addressable by *this specific product* within *this specific go-to-market motion*?

### Layer 6: Defensibility & Competitive Position

**What to assess:** In AI, defensibility is no longer rooted in tech stack alone. EY's January 2026 analysis concluded that "defensibility now rests in proprietary data, customer ecosystems, and operational embedding rather than tech stack alone" [E].

**The four moats that matter in AI (2026):**

1. **Data moat** — Proprietary, compounding datasets that improve with usage
2. **Workflow embedding** — Product so deep in customer operations that switching costs are prohibitive
3. **Network effects** — Each user makes the product better for all users
4. **Regulatory moat** — Compliance certifications (SOC2, HIPAA, EU AI Act) that take 12–18 months to obtain

**What is NOT a moat:**
- "We were first" — irrelevant if the product is replicable in a weekend with the latest foundation model
- "Our prompt engineering" — this is not IP, it's a text file
- "We have a partnership with [Big Tech]" — partnerships are revocable. Anthropic blocked Windsurf [E]. Platform risk is existential for wrappers.

### Layer 7: AI Governance & Trust Infrastructure

**What to assess:** This is the layer most investors skip and will regret skipping. As AI moves from experimental to production-critical, governance becomes a deal qualifier.

**Key questions:**
- Does the company have documented model evaluation procedures?
- Is there bias testing, output monitoring, and audit logging?
- How is PII handled in training data and inference?
- Is there a responsible AI policy, and can the CTO explain it without reading from a document?
- EU AI Act compliance readiness?

**Why it matters for valuation:** CLA Connect's 2026 predictions state that "responsible AI frameworks covering bias, transparency, and governance will be non-negotiable" [E] in PE deals. Companies that treat governance as an afterthought will face valuation haircuts at exit.

## 4. Red Flags That Kill Deals

These are patterns I've seen repeatedly across deal flow at 36ZERO Capital and through VC Lab cohort evaluation. Each one has cost real investors real money.

### Red Flag #1: The "Contracted Revenue" Illusion
AI startups frequently cite "$10M in contracted revenue" that, upon deeper inspection, represents year-three projections from non-binding LOIs [E]. Healthcare AI investors specifically flag "squishy revenue metrics" as a top deal-killer [E]. **Fix:** Ask for paid invoices from the last 90 days. Not contracts. Not LOIs. Invoices.

### Red Flag #2: The Undisclosed API Dependency
The startup's "proprietary AI" turns out to be a thin wrapper around OpenAI's API with minimal fine-tuning [I]. They have no defensible data moat, their unit economics collapse if API costs increase, and they are one model update away from commoditization. **Fix:** Request the architecture diagram. Count the number of external API calls per user transaction.

### Red Flag #3: The Phantom ML Team
The org chart shows 40 employees. The ML team is 2 people, one of whom is a data analyst reclassified as "AI Engineer" for fundraising purposes [J]. **Fix:** Interview the technical team separately from founders. Ask them to explain the training pipeline.

### Red Flag #4: Emergency Bridge Requests
"We need to close this round in two weeks." Urgency in fundraising is almost always a sign of cash mismanagement, not market opportunity [I]. Builder.ai's collapse demonstrated how emergency bridge requests and sudden executive churn are early-warning signals that compound when ignored [E].

### Red Flag #5: No Model Evaluation Framework
The company cannot tell you how they measure model performance, what their baseline accuracy is, or how they detect model drift. If the CTO says "we check it manually," walk away [J].

### Red Flag #6: The Pivot-Into-AI
The company was doing something else 18 months ago. Now they're "AI-first." Their codebase still runs the old product. The AI features are bolted on, not architecturally integrated [J]. **Fix:** Look at the git history. When did the first ML-related commit appear?

### Red Flag #7: Customer Concentration + AI Dependency
One customer represents 40%+ of revenue, and that customer could build the same capability in-house with a weekend hackathon using the same APIs [J]. This is not a business—it's a consulting engagement with a software wrapper.

## 5. The Wrapper Test: 5 Questions to Separate Real AI from Marketing

Before writing a term sheet, run every AI deal through these five questions. If the answer to three or more is "yes," you're looking at a wrapper, not a company.

### Question 1: "Can I rebuild the core product in a weekend using publicly available APIs?"
If a competent developer could replicate 80% of the product's functionality using OpenAI's API, Langchain, and a standard database, the startup has no technical moat. The remaining 20% better be extraordinary. [J]

### Question 2: "What happens to the product if their primary AI provider doubles pricing?"
Run the unit economics at 2x current API costs. If margins go negative, the business model is structurally dependent on someone else's pricing decisions. About 70% [J] of AI application-layer startups I evaluate fail this test.

### Question 3: "Does the company generate proprietary data that makes the product better over time?"
This is the single most important question. A data flywheel—where product usage generates data that improves the model that improves the product—is the only durable source of competitive advantage in application-layer AI [I]. Without it, the company is renting intelligence, not building it.

### Question 4: "Can the CTO explain the model architecture without slides?"
Technical depth manifests in conversation, not in pitch decks. If the CTO can whiteboard the full pipeline—data ingestion, preprocessing, model architecture, training loop, evaluation metrics, deployment infrastructure—they're building something real. If they defer to "we use state-of-the-art models," that's a marketing statement, not a technology strategy [J].

### Question 5: "Is the AI the product, or is it a feature?"
Many startups label themselves "AI companies" when AI is a feature within a larger product. That's not inherently bad—but it changes the valuation framework entirely. An AI company should be valued on AI-specific multiples. A software company with AI features should be valued on SaaS multiples with an AI premium. The difference can be 5–10x [I] in valuation outcomes.

| **EV/EBITDA (public AI/robotics)** | 15.8x–16.8x [E] | Median range through 2025, trending up in Q4 |
| **ARR threshold for PE interest** | $5M–$10M+ [I] | Below this, most institutional investors won't engage |

### What Drives Premium Multiples?

1. **Proprietary model architecture** — Not using off-the-shelf. Custom training, custom inference. (+3–5x on base multiple) [I]
2. **Data flywheel in motion** — Demonstrable improvement in model performance correlated with user growth. (+2–4x) [I]
3. **Regulatory certification** — SOC2, HIPAA, EU AI Act compliance. (+1–2x) [J]
4. **Net Revenue Retention >130%** — Proves stickiness and expansion. (+2–3x) [I]
5. **Declining inference cost curve** — Shows engineering capability and margin expansion potential. (+1–3x) [I]

### What Kills Multiples?

1. **API wrapper architecture** — Investors increasingly apply a "wrapper discount" of 30–50% [J] to companies with no proprietary model layer
2. **Customer concentration** — Top customer >25% of revenue. (-2–3x) [I]
3. **Negative gross margin trajectory** — Inference costs growing faster than revenue. Immediate pass.
4. **No clear path to profitability** — OpenAI can burn $5B/year because it has $500B in backing [E]. Your Series A startup cannot.

### Reference Valuations (2025–2026)

| Company | Valuation | Revenue/ARR | Implied Multiple | Notes |

## 7. Case Study: How We'd Evaluate [Anonymized Company — "MedAssist AI"]

**Company profile:** MedAssist AI is a Series A healthcare startup claiming to use AI for clinical decision support. They report $3.2M ARR, 150% YoY growth, and say they serve 45 hospital systems.

### Layer-by-Layer Assessment

**Layer 1 — Tech Stack:** MedAssist uses GPT-4 via API for natural language processing of clinical notes, combined with a proprietary classification model trained on 2.3M labeled clinical encounters. The proprietary model handles the diagnostic logic; the LLM handles the NLP preprocessing. **Verdict:** Hybrid architecture. Not a pure wrapper, but LLM dependency creates platform risk. Score: 3.5/5 [J].

**Layer 2 — Data Moat:** The 2.3M labeled clinical encounters are sourced through exclusive data-sharing agreements with three hospital networks. Each new deployment adds data. The flywheel is real but early-stage. **Verdict:** Genuine data asset with compounding potential. Score: 4/5 [J].

**Layer 3 — Team:** 32 employees. 11 ML engineers (34% of headcount). CTO has 8 years in clinical NLP, published in peer-reviewed journals. Chief Medical Officer is a practicing physician. No single point of failure—three senior ML engineers can independently manage the training pipeline. **Verdict:** Strong, balanced team. Score: 4.5/5 [J].

**Layer 4 — Unit Economics:** 62% gross margin (below SaaS benchmarks but typical for inference-heavy AI). Inference cost per clinical note: $0.12, declining at 15% per quarter through model optimization. CAC payback: 14 months. NRR: 135%. **Verdict:** Healthy for the stage. Margin expansion path is clear. Score: 4/5 [J].

**Layer 5 — Market:** Clinical decision support market is estimated at $7.5B by 2028 [I]. Regulatory environment (FDA, EU MDR) creates barriers to entry but also slows go-to-market. MedAssist has FDA 510(k) clearance pending. **Verdict:** Large market with regulatory moat potential. Timing is right. Score: 4/5 [J].

**Layer 6 — Defensibility:** Data moat (exclusive hospital agreements) + regulatory moat (FDA pathway) + workflow embedding (integrated into EHR systems). Switching costs are high once deployed. **Verdict:** Multiple reinforcing moats. Score: 4.5/5 [J].

**Layer 7 — Governance:** Documented bias testing across demographic groups. Audit logging for all model outputs. HIPAA compliant. Responsible AI policy reviewed quarterly by clinical advisory board. **Verdict:** Above average for the stage. Score: 4/5 [J].

### Wrapper Test Results
1. Can I rebuild in a weekend? — No. Proprietary classification model requires domain-specific labeled data.
2. What if API costs double? — Margins compress to ~48%. Painful but survivable. They're actively working on self-hosted inference.
3. Proprietary data flywheel? — Yes. Each deployment adds labeled clinical encounters.
4. CTO whiteboard test? — Passed. Detailed explanation of multi-stage pipeline without notes.
5. AI as product or feature? — AI is the product. Clinical decision support doesn't exist without the model.

**Wrapper Test: 0/5 flags. This is a real AI company.**

### Valuation Assessment
- Comparable revenue multiple range: 20–35x ARR [I] for healthcare AI with regulatory clearance
- Applied multiple: 28x on $3.2M ARR = **$89.6M pre-money valuation** [J]
- Discount factors: FDA clearance still pending (-10%), hospital sales cycle concentration risk (-5%)
- **Adjusted valuation range: $75–90M** [J]

## 8. The AI Trust Layer as Differentiator

Here's a thesis most investors aren't pricing in yet: **Trust infrastructure is the next moat in AI.**

As AI systems move from decision-support to decision-making, the question shifts from "Does it work?" to "Can we prove it works, and to whom?" This creates a new infrastructure layer—the trust layer—that encompasses:

- **Explainability:** Can the system explain why it made a specific recommendation?
- **Auditability:** Is there a complete, tamper-resistant log of every model input, output, and decision?
- **Compliance automation:** Can the system automatically adapt to regulatory requirements across jurisdictions (EU AI Act, FDA, HIPAA, SOC2)?
- **Bias monitoring:** Real-time detection and alerting for model drift and demographic bias.

### Why This Matters for Investment Thesis

Companies building trust infrastructure are positioned similarly to where cybersecurity companies were in 2015—before every enterprise buyer made security a procurement requirement. The EU AI Act is accelerating this shift. By 2027, every AI system deployed in a high-risk category (healthcare, finance, HR, legal) will need demonstrable compliance with transparency and accountability requirements [E].

**The investment opportunity:** Companies that embed trust infrastructure into their core product—not as an afterthought, but as architecture—will command valuation premiums as regulatory requirements tighten. Companies that build trust infrastructure as a *platform* for other AI companies face a TAM that scales with the entire AI application layer [J].

**Current gap:** Fewer than 15% [J] of AI startups in my deal flow have meaningful governance infrastructure beyond a policy document. This is both a risk (for those companies) and an opportunity (for investors identifying the trust-layer winners early).

### What to Look For

1. **Model cards and documentation** — Does the company publish standardized documentation for each model's capabilities, limitations, and appropriate use cases?
2. **Continuous evaluation pipelines** — Not just pre-deployment testing, but ongoing monitoring of model performance in production.
3. **Regulatory roadmap** — A concrete plan for EU AI Act compliance, not "we'll figure it out when we need to."
4. **Customer-facing transparency** — Can end-users understand and challenge AI-driven decisions?

The trust layer is where the next $100B+ company gets built [A]. The infrastructure for proving AI works is as valuable as the AI itself.
