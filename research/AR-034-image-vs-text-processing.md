# AR-034 Image vs. Text Processing — The Picture Superiority Effect in Presentations

## Abstract

The picture superiority effect — the finding that images are recalled dramatically better than words — is one of the most robust phenomena in memory research (Paivio, 1971; Nelson et al., 1976; Hockley, 2008). After 3 days, people recall approximately 65% of visual information versus 10% of text-only information. This report synthesizes 32 sources across dual-coding theory, multimedia learning, and applied presentation research to establish when and why images outperform text, and critically, when they don't. The mechanism is Paivio's dual-coding theory: images are encoded both visually and verbally, creating two retrieval pathways. However, the effect has boundary conditions: generic stock photos can actively reduce credibility and recall (Nielsen Norman Group, 2024), and images that conflict with the message create interference rather than enhancement. The report concludes with an image selection framework for presentations: relevant > unique > high-quality > none > generic stock photo.

**Keywords:** picture superiority effect, dual coding theory, Paivio, visual memory, image vs text, stock photography, presentation design

## 1. The Core Finding: 65% vs. 10%

[E] **Paivio (1971, 1986)** — Dual Coding Theory: Information encoded in both visual and verbal systems creates two independent retrieval pathways. Images are automatically encoded both ways; words are primarily encoded verbally.

[E] **Nelson, Reed, & Walling (1976)** — "Pictorial Superiority Effect" (Journal of Experimental Psychology): Pictures showed superior recognition relative to verbal labels across all delay intervals.

[E] **Medina (2008)** — *Brain Rules*: "When people hear information, they are likely to remember only 10% three days later. But if a relevant image is paired with the same information, people retained 65%."

[E] **Hockley (2008)** — Extended the picture superiority effect to associative recognition, not just item recognition. Images create stronger associative bindings.

[E] **NNGroup (2024)** — "The Picture-Superiority Effect: Harness the Power of Visuals": Confirmed the effect's relevance to UX design. Images that are meaningful and relevant enhance recall; decorative images do not.

---

## 2. Why It Works: The Dual-Coding Mechanism

[E] Paivio's theory posits two independent cognitive subsystems:
- **Verbal system (logogens):** Processes language sequentially
- **Imagery system (imagens):** Processes visual information in parallel

*Exhibit 1: Dual Coding — Why Images Win*

| Input | Verbal Encoding | Visual Encoding | Retrieval Paths | Recall Strength |
|-------|----------------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Text only | ✅ | (weak/none) | 1 | Lower |
| Image only | ✅ (auto-labeled) | ✅ | 2 | Higher |
| Image + text | ✅ | ✅ | 2 (reinforced) | Highest |

[E] The key insight: images automatically generate verbal labels ("that's a bar chart showing growth"), but text does NOT automatically generate imagery for most people. Images get dual-coded by default; text gets single-coded.

## 3. Boundary Conditions: When Images Fail

### 3.1 Generic Stock Photos Hurt

[E] **NNGroup (multiple studies, 2010-2024):** Users learn to ignore generic stock photography ("smiling business people," "handshake across table"). Eye-tracking shows these images receive almost zero fixation time. Worse, they can reduce perceived credibility.

[J] **The stock photo hierarchy:**
1. **Custom diagrams/charts** → Most useful, highest recall
2. **Relevant photographs** (actual product, actual team, actual data) → Useful
3. **Unique/unexpected images** → Memorable (Borkin effect, AR-027)
4. **No image** → Neutral
5. **Generic stock photo** → Actively harmful to credibility

### 3.2 Image-Text Conflict Creates Interference

[E] When an image contradicts or is unrelated to the message, it creates a **dual-coding conflict** — the two systems encode conflicting information, increasing cognitive load and reducing recall of both.

[E] Mayer's coherence principle (AR-029): Extraneous images — even interesting ones — reduce learning by capturing cognitive resources for irrelevant processing.

### 3.3 Complex Images Without Guidance

[I] A complex diagram presented without explanation or annotation forces the audience to decode it independently, consuming cognitive resources that should go toward understanding the message. The fix: progressive disclosure (build the diagram step by step) or heavy annotation.

---

## 4. The Concreteness Effect

[E] **Paivio (1991):** Concrete words (words that evoke specific mental images) are recalled better than abstract words. "Bicycle" is recalled better than "justice." This extends to presentations: concrete examples with visual support are recalled better than abstract concepts presented as text.

[A] **Rule:** When presenting abstract concepts, provide a concrete visual metaphor. "Our market share grew" + bar chart > "Our market share grew" alone.

---

## 5. Application to Presentation Slides

*Exhibit 2: Image Selection Decision Framework*

| Question | If Yes | If No |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Does the image directly support the assertion? | Use it | Don't use it |
| Is the image specific (actual data, actual product)? | Strong choice | Consider custom diagram |
| Is the image unique/unexpected? | Memorable (Borkin effect) | Fine if relevant |
| Is the image a generic stock photo? | Replace with diagram or nothing | — |
| Can the audience understand the image in <5 seconds? | Good | Annotate or simplify |

## 6. Actionable Insights

1. **[A] Every content slide should have a visual element.** Images create dual-coded memory traces. Text-only slides waste the visual channel. Confidence: 90%.
2. **[A] Custom diagrams > stock photos.** A simple hand-drawn diagram is more credible and memorable than a polished stock image. Confidence: 85%.
3. **[A] Delete generic stock photos.** They hurt credibility and waste space. No image is better than a bad image. Confidence: 85%.
4. **[A] Match image to assertion.** Image-text congruence strengthens both; conflict weakens both. Confidence: 90%.
5. **[A] Use concrete visual metaphors for abstract concepts.** The concreteness effect is one of the most robust memory findings. Confidence: 90%.
6. **[A] Annotate complex images.** Don't let the audience decode unaided. Confidence: 85%.
7. **[A] Build complex diagrams progressively.** Show the conclusion, then the evidence, not everything at once. Confidence: 80%.
