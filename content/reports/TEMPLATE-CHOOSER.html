0. Report Definition

The strategic foundation. Who reads this, what they expect, what the report must achieve.

Primary Audience: CEO, Board Director, General Counsel

  • They expect: Strategic implications in 2 pages. Numbers with sources. Clear "so what" per section.
  • They're convinced by: Risk quantification ($, %), regulatory deadlines, peer company comparisons, actionable recommendations.
  • They're repelled by: Jargon without context, excessive length, hedging without conclusion, "AI hype" language.

Secondary Audience: CTO, VP Engineering, Universities/Academics

  • They expect: Technical depth with methodology transparency. Reproducible claims. Framework-level thinking.
  • They're convinced by: Peer-reviewed sources, architectural specifics, calibration methodology, honest limitations.
  • They're repelled by: Surface-level analysis, missing methodology, claims without confidence levels, marketing tone.

Tertiary: Team Leads, Analysts

  • They expect: Checklists, frameworks, self-assessments they can use Monday morning.
  • They're convinced by: Concrete tools (scoring rubrics, decision trees), real company examples, cost estimates.
  • They're repelled by: Abstract theory without application, no clear next steps.

Report Objective & Differentiator

  • Objective: After reading, the reader should change one decision about how they deploy, govern, or invest in AI agents.
  • Tone: Professional, evidence-based, honest about limitations. No "I reviewed/analyzed/examined."
  • Differentiator: Confidence methodology (calibrated, not self-reported), Transparency Note (Beipackzettel), Claim Register (every number traceable).
  • What we're NOT: Not a whitepaper (too shallow), not a journal paper (too narrow), not a McKinsey deck (too polished, not transparent enough).
