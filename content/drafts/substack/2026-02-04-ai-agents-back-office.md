## What Actually Works

**Research is the killer app.**

When I needed to evaluate 10 VC funds for applications, a research agent analyzed each fund's thesis, recent investments, team backgrounds, and portfolio overlap with my experience. Output: 10 personalized cover letters with specific references to their portfolio companies.

Time: 15 minutes. Doing this manually: 2 full days.

**Drafting at scale works.**

20 personalized outreach emails for CNC manufacturing companies in Saxony, each referencing the company's specific machinery, team size, and pain points. Not mail-merge garbage — genuinely personalized.

**Analysis works.**

An agent read 40+ files in my Obsidian vault, identified gaps in my knowledge base, found two unpublished blog drafts I'd forgotten about, and suggested which ones to prioritize based on trending topics.

**Content repurposing works.**

One Substack article becomes a LinkedIn post, a Twitter thread, three Substack Notes, and a WhatsApp message to my VC Lab cohort. Same ideas, different formats, done in minutes.

## What Doesn't Work

**Judgment calls.**

AI doesn't know which email to send first. It doesn't know that the uncle's business contact is warmer than a cold lead from a Google search. It doesn't understand that Tuesday morning is better than Friday evening for outreach in German manufacturing. Context is still human.

**Quality without constraints.**

Left to its own devices, AI produces "technically complete" work that no human would actually use. A presentation with compressed margins. A landing page with three missing closing tags. A pricing page that changed three times because I kept giving vague feedback.

The fix: detailed quality gates, checklists, and expectations — defined before the agent starts. Not after. Garbage in, garbage out is more true for AI than for any human employee.

**Relationship building.**

AI can research a person. It can draft a personalized message. It cannot build trust, read body language in a meeting, or know when to shut up and listen. The most important parts of business are still fundamentally human.

**Pressing send.**

This is the uncomfortable one.

## The Human Bottleneck

Here are my actual numbers from last week:

| Metric | Prepared | Sent |
|--------|----------|------|
| Outreach emails | 20 | 0 |
| VC applications | 10 | 0 |
| LinkedIn posts | 10 | 1 |
| Content pieces | 15 | 1 |

Prepared-to-sent ratio: **55:2**.

AI didn't fail me. I failed me.

The bottleneck was never research, writing, or analysis. It was always the decision to put something in front of another human being. AI makes this worse, not better — because now you have no excuse. The work is done. The only thing left is you.

I calculated the opportunity cost of this gap: roughly €421 per day in unsent outreach. Since January 1st, that's ~€14,700 left on the table.

AI is a mirror. It shows you exactly where your real problems are. And they're rarely technical.

## The Playbook (If You Want to Try This)

**Start small.** Don't build 10 agents on day one. Start with one: research. Have AI research your next meeting, your next client, your next job application. See if the output is useful.

**Define expectations before you start.** "Write me an email" produces garbage. "Write a 5-sentence email to a CNC manufacturing company owner in Saxony, referencing their Heidenhain machines, offering a free 30-minute demo of automated work preparation software" produces something usable.

**Build quality gates.** A checklist that every output must pass before it reaches you. No placeholder text. No unverified claims. No broken links. Sounds obvious. AI skips all of these by default.

**Measure outcomes, not outputs.** I tracked "emails written" for a week and felt productive. Then I tracked "emails sent" and felt sick. The metric that matters is always the one that reaches another human being.

**Press send.** Seriously. The AI did the work. Your job is to deliver it.
