## üéØ Top 10 Pitch Deck Frameworks

### 1. **Sequoia Capital Format**
**Created by:** Sequoia Capital (one of the world's top VC firms)  
**Structure:** 10 core slides  
**Best for:** B2B SaaS, tech startups seeking Series A+

**Slides:**
1. Company Purpose
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Why Now
5. Market Size
6. Competition
7. Product
8. Business Model
9. Team
10. Financials

**Key Insight:** "Why now?" is critical - timing can make or break a startup.

**Source:** [sequoiacap.com](https://www.sequoiacap.com/)

---

### 2. **Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 Rule**
**Created by:** Guy Kawasaki (ex-Apple, VC, author)  
**Structure:** 10 slides, 20 minutes, 30-point font  
**Best for:** Any startup, especially first-time founders

**The Rule:**
- **10 slides maximum** - Forces clarity and focus
- **20 minutes max** - Respect investors' time
- **30-point font minimum** - Prevents text overload

**Slides:**
1. Title
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Business Model
5. Underlying Magic (tech/secret sauce)
6. Marketing & Sales
7. Competition
8. Team
9. Projections & Milestones
10. Status & Timeline

**Key Insight:** If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.

---

### 3. **Y Combinator (YC) Format**
**Created by:** Y Combinator (world's top accelerator)  
**Structure:** Lean, demo-focused  
**Best for:** Early-stage startups (pre-seed, seed)

**Core Elements:**
1. Company one-liner (what you do in 10 words)
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Traction (most important!)
5. Market size
6. Business model
7. Team
8. Competition
9. Ask

**Key Insight:** YC obsesses over **traction**. Show growth, revenue, users - not just ideas.

**Source:** [ycombinator.com](https://www.ycombinator.com/)

---

### 4. **Airbnb Format (Problem-First)**
**Created by:** Airbnb founders  
**Structure:** Classic 10-slide with heavy emphasis on problem validation  
**Best for:** Consumer marketplaces, platforms

**What Made It Work:**
- Started with relatable problem (expensive hotels, impersonal)
- Showed market size with simple math
- Used photos/mockups instead of walls of text
- Validated with early user stories

**Key Insight:** Make the problem so clear that investors feel it themselves.

---

### 5. **Peter Thiel's Contrarian Framework**
**Created by:** Peter Thiel (PayPal, Palantir, Founders Fund)  
**Structure:** Question-driven  
**Best for:** Deep tech, moonshot startups

**Key Questions to Answer:**
1. What important truth do very few people agree with you on?
2. Can you create a monopoly in a small market first?
3. Is this a 10x improvement, not 10% better?
4. Can you defend your advantage?
5. Do you have proprietary technology?

**Key Insight:** "Competition is for losers." Build a monopoly in a niche, then expand.

---

### 6. **DocSend Data-Driven Format**
**Created by:** DocSend research (analyzing 1000s of decks)  
**Structure:** Based on what actually gets funded  
**Best for:** Data-informed founders

**Optimal Structure (based on what VCs spend time on):**
1. **Intro** (47 seconds avg)
2. **Problem** (2min 13sec avg - 2nd most time)

## üö´ Common Mistakes (Top 20)

Based on analysis of 82+ pitch deck reviews and feedback from VCs.

### **1. Too Wordy (74/82 decks make this mistake)**
- **Problem:** Walls of text. Your pitch deck is not a novel.
- **Fix:** One idea per slide. Use visuals. If you need more than 3 bullets, it's too much.

### **2. No Clear Problem Statement**
- **Problem:** Investors can't understand what pain you're solving
- **Fix:** Make the problem visceral and relatable. Use real stories or data.

### **3. Burying the Traction Slide**
- **Problem:** Hiding your progress at slide 15
- **Fix:** If you have traction, put it in the first 3-5 slides

### **4. Unrealistic Market Size**
- **Problem:** "$10 trillion TAM" with no path to capture it
- **Fix:** Show TAM ‚Üí SAM ‚Üí SOM. Be honest about what you can realistically capture.

### **5. No Clear Differentiation**
- **Problem:** "We're like Uber but for X" without explaining why you'll win
- **Fix:** Show your unfair advantage. Why can't competitors copy you in 6 months?

### **6. Weak Team Slide**
- **Problem:** Listing job titles without showing relevant expertise
- **Fix:** Highlight domain expertise, previous exits, complementary skills

### **7. Missing Financial Projections**
- **Problem:** No revenue model or unrealistic hockey stick
- **Fix:** Show unit economics. Be realistic but ambitious.

### **8. Too Technical / Jargon-Heavy**
- **Problem:** "We leverage AI/ML to synergize blockchain-enabled solutions"
- **Fix:** Explain it like you're talking to your smart friend who's not in tech

### **9. Ignoring Competition**
- **Problem:** "We have no competitors" (red flag!)
- **Fix:** Show competitive landscape honestly. Explain why you'll win.

### **10. No Clear Ask**
- **Problem:** Ending without stating how much you're raising and why
- **Fix:** "We're raising $X for Y months runway to achieve Z milestones"

### **11. Inconsistent Design**
- **Problem:** Mix of fonts, colors, styles - looks unprofessional
- **Fix:** Use a template. Keep branding consistent. Clean > flashy.

### **12. Forgetting Contact Info**
- **Problem:** Investor wants to follow up... but can't find your email
- **Fix:** Put contact info on the last slide (and in deck metadata)

### **13. Too Many Slides**
- **Problem:** 40-slide deck that loses investor attention
- **Fix:** 10-20 slides max. Have appendix for detailed info.

### **14. No Demo or Product Screenshots**
- **Problem:** Describing your product without showing it
- **Fix:** Include actual product screenshots, demo video, or prototype

### **15. Unclear Business Model**
- **Problem:** "We'll figure out monetization later"
- **Fix:** Show at least 2-3 revenue stream ideas with assumptions

### **16. Poor Storytelling Flow**
- **Problem:** Jumping randomly between topics
- **Fix:** Follow a logical narrative: Problem ‚Üí Solution ‚Üí Market ‚Üí Why Us ‚Üí Ask

### **17. Not Addressing Risks**
- **Problem:** Pretending there are no challenges
- **Fix:** Acknowledge 1-2 key risks and show mitigation plan

### **18. Outdated Data**
- **Problem:** Using traction metrics from 6 months ago
- **Fix:** Update your deck monthly with latest numbers

### **19. Weak Opening Slide**
- **Problem:** Generic "About Us" that doesn't hook attention
- **Fix:** Use a bold one-liner or compelling problem stat

### **20. Sending PDF Instead of Using Analytics Tools**
- **Problem:** No idea if investors read your deck or which slides they spent time on
- **Fix:** Use DocSend, Pitch, or similar tools to track engagement

## üìä What VCs Actually Look At First

Based on DocSend research analyzing investor behavior across 1000s of pitch decks.

### **Time Spent Per Deck**
- **Average total time:** 3 minutes 44 seconds
- **Median time:** 2 minutes 45 seconds
- **Successful decks:** ~4 minutes
- **Rejected decks:** ~2 minutes

**Insight:** You have less than 4 minutes to make an impression.

---

### **Slides VCs Spend Most Time On**

1. **Financials** - 2 min 13 sec average
   - Revenue projections
   - Unit economics
   - Burn rate and runway
   - **Why:** Shows if the business is fundable

2. **Team** - 1 min 47 sec average
   - Founder backgrounds
   - Relevant experience
   - Previous exits
   - **Why:** VCs bet on people, not just ideas

3. **Competition** - 1 min 27 sec average
   - Competitive landscape
   - Market positioning
   - Differentiation
   - **Why:** Need to understand if you can win

4. **Product** - 1 min 20 sec average
   - Screenshots
   - Demo
   - Features
   - **Why:** Is this actually built?

5. **Problem/Solution** - 54 sec average each
   - Market pain point
   - Your fix
   - **Why:** Core thesis validation

---

### **First Impressions Matter**

**Opening slide views:**
- 100% of investors view slide 1
- 75% view slide 2
- 50% view slide 3
- Drop-off continues...

**Insight:** Hook them in the first 30 seconds or lose them.

---

### **Optimal Deck Length**

| Slide Count | Avg. Time Spent | Funding Success Rate |
|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| 10-15 slides | 4m 12s | Highest |
| 16-20 slides | 3m 44s | Good |
| 21-25 slides | 3m 10s | Declining |
| 26+ slides | 2m 30s | Lowest |

**Insight:** 10-20 slides is the sweet spot. Longer decks get skimmed.

---

### **Attachment Engagement**

- **Decks sent with** a personalized message: 50% higher engagement
- **Decks sent Monday-Thursday**: Better open rates than Friday-Sunday
- **Follow-up timing**: Wait 3-5 days before first follow-up

---

### **What Gets Shared Internally**

VCs forward ~40% of decks to partners. Decks that get shared have:
- Clear one-liner on slide 1
- Strong traction metrics
- Impressive team credentials
- Big market opportunity
- Clear differentiation

---

### **Key Investor Signals**

**Positive signals:**
- Viewing deck 2+ times
- Spending >5 minutes total
- Downloading the deck
- Forwarding to others
- Viewing appendix/detailed slides

**Negative signals:**
- Viewing <2 minutes
- Skipping team slide
- Not finishing the deck
- No follow-up within 7 days

---

### **What VCs Say vs. What Data Shows**

| VCs Say They Care About | Data Shows They Actually Spend Time On |
|-------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Vision & Mission | Financials & Traction |
| Market Size | Team Background |
| Innovation | Competition Analysis |
| Long-term Strategy | Near-term Revenue Model |

**Insight:** VCs are pattern-matching. Show them what they're used to seeing work.

**SaaS:** VCs spend most time on MRR, churn, CAC/LTV  
**Consumer:** DAU/MAU, retention, viral coefficient  
**Biotech/Hardware:** Team credentials, IP, regulatory path  
**Marketplace:** GMV, take rate, supply/demand balance
